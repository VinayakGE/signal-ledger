## What Was Most Important

Not: Opening WeWork Investigation #003

**But:** Downgrading Meta-Hypothesis M1 BEFORE evidence arrived

This was the discipline working.

- Prediction made
- Evidence arriving
- Prediction revised downward in confidence
- Status recorded as "Emerging, Attackable, Unvalidated"

This is what Phase 2 should demand of every hypothesis.

Including its own.

## The Danger That Was Identified

**Risk:** WeWork accidentally becoming a methodology test instead of a real investigation

**How it happens:**
- Phase 2 objects become interesting (PP#002, Zone A/B, Evidence Classes, M1)
- Investigation is guided by "Do objects survive?"
- WeWork stops being about WeWork; becomes about testing frameworks
- Repository starts protecting objects instead of learning from them
- Methodology becomes religion

**Why it matters:**
- Same error Phase 1 made with explanations (defending instead of testing)
- Inverts the whole point of Phase 2 (learning what survives, not proving predictions)
- Repository would be doing the opposite of what it disciplines itself to do

---

## The Signal That Was Observed

**Every surviving object is about INVESTIGATION, not organizations:**

- Evidence Class Taxonomy: How to classify evidence in investigations
- PP#002: How to observe organizational selections while motivations remain opaque
- Zone A/B Boundary: What makes something knowable vs. unknowable
- M1: What kinds of knowledge survive adversarial pressure

**Not:**
- "Why did WeWork fail?"
- "What causes organizational failures?"
- "What decisions led to collapse?"

**But:**
- "What can we know and not know about why organizations fail?"
- "How do we systematically investigate when internal motivation is private?"
- "Which kinds of knowledge survive the test of having predictions checked?"

**Signal:** The repository is discovering **investigation methodology**, not **organizational explanations**.

This may be what it's actually good at.

---

## The Counterintuitive Highest-Value Outcome

**Most people would think:** "All three Phase 2 objects survive! Methodology validated!"

**More sophisticated view:** "Finding a durable explanatory object in WeWork would be more valuable than another surviving boundary."

**Why:**
- Another boundary confirms pattern (predictable, marginal info gain)
- Durable explanation wounds M1 (unpredictable, high info gain)
- Teaches WHEN explanations survive (currently: Phase 2 assumes they don't)
- Answers the real question: Which kinds of knowledge survive?

**This reorientation shifts success from "confirmation" to "learning"**

---

## The Question That Matters Most

**Not:** "What result would validate the repository most?"

**But:** "What result would surprise the repository most?"

These produce opposite search strategies:

**Validation-seeking:**
- Looks for confirming evidence
- Dismisses contradictions
- Rationalizes away wounds
- Methodology self-admiration

**Surprise-seeking:**
- Looks for unexpected patterns
- Investigates contradictions
- Welcomes wounds (high info value)
- Learning from methodology

**Phase 2 should pursue surprise, not validation.**

---

## What This Means for WeWork

### Reoriented Priorities

**TIER 1 (Investigation):**
- What is most surprising about WeWork?
- What does WeWork reveal about investigation itself?
- What remains unknowable despite extensive public record?

**TIER 2 (Objects):**
- Do Phase 2 objects survive or die?
- Are objects universal or domain-specific?

**TIER 3 (Meta):**
- What is the repository discovering about which kinds of knowledge survive?

**Tier 1 gets answered first. Tier 2 and 3 emerge from Tier 1 findings.**

### The Guard Rail

**Before asking:** "Does this wound PP#002?"

**Always first ask:** "Is this the most important finding WeWork is trying to tell us?"

If the answer to the second question is "No, something else is more important," then the something-else takes priority — even if it doesn't relate to Phase 2 objects.

Possible answers:

## The Shift in Values

### From (Implicit):
- "Does our methodology work?" (protective question)
- "Do our objects survive?" (confirmation question)
- "What validates our predictions?" (validation question)

### To (Explicit):
- "What does investigation reveal?" (discovery question)
- "Which objects survive? Which don't? Why?" (learning question)
- "What surprises us about what survives?" (surprise question)

**This is the difference between defending and learning.**

## What Success Looks Like

**Not:** "Phase 2 objects all survived; methodology validated"

**But:** "WeWork revealed X (most surprising finding). Phase 2 objects performed Y. This teaches us Z about which kinds of knowledge survive investigation."

Both outcomes valuable:
- Objects survive → Objects are robust
- Objects wounded → Objects are domain-specific (still valuable)
- Mix of both → Most realistic (some robust, some limited)
- Something unexpected → Highest value (repository learning something new)
