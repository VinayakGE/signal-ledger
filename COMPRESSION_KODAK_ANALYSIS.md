# Kodak Compression Analysis — PP#002 and Zone A/B Boundary Test

**Date:** 2026-06-23

**Question:** Does Kodak show the same Observable Selection / Opaque Motivation structure as BlackBerry and GE?

---

## Kodak's Observable Structure (Zone B)

### Primary Observable Fact: Margin Collapse Despite Stable Revenue

**Timeline:**
- 2000: Operating margin ~20%, revenue $10B
- 2005: Operating margin ~15%, revenue $10B (signal visible: margins declining while revenue flat)
- 2008: Operating margin <5%, revenue $10B (signal clear: significant cost-structure problem)
- 2012: Bankruptcy

**Observation:** Margins compressed from 20% to 5% over 8 years while revenue remained relatively flat ($10B ± 15%). This is structurally different from revenue collapse. The problem is not "customers stopped buying" but rather "profitability decayed while sales remained stable."

**What this observable fact tells us:** The organization faced an economic problem (cost structure exceeding revenue base) and the organization recognized it. Evidence:

1. **R&D Investment Signal:** $3B+ cumulative R&D spending 1993-2005 on digital initiatives
2. **Strategic Announcement:** 2005 explicit pivot announced: "Digital revenue to exceed traditional"
3. **Cost-structure actions:** Multiple restructurings, headcount reductions, facility closures documented

**What this observable fact does NOT tell us:** Why the margin decline persisted despite R&D investment and strategic announcements. Why the pivot was announced but not executed proportionally.

---

## Kodak's Selection: The Divergence Between Signal and Response

### Selection #1: R&D Investment Without Proportional Revenue Scaling

**Observable:**
- R&D spending: ~$3B+ cumulative 1993-2005
- R&D output: Digital imaging revenue <10% of total by 2005-2010
- Margin outcome: Digital revenue did not scale; film revenue declined; margins compressed

**Selection visible:** Kodak selected to invest heavily in digital while maintaining film business protection. This is observable choice.

**Alternative selection NOT made:** Kodak did not divest film business entirely to fund digital scale-up. Kodak did not pivot to digital-only platform.

**Why this selection matters:** Margin signal (20% → 5%) is compatible with two different strategic choices:
1. **Digital-first pivot pursued:** Margins compress temporarily during transition (expected H3 execution-failure pattern)
2. **Film-protection strategy sustained:** Margins compress structurally (suggests H4 or H5 pattern)

Kodak chose #2, but the evidence does not show whether this was conviction or constraint.

### Selection #2: Strategic Announcement Without Proportional Organizational Response

**Observable:**
- 2005: Announced "Digital revenue to exceed traditional"
- 2005-2010: Digital revenue remained <10% of total
- 2010: Still primarily film-dependent at bankruptcy

**Selection visible:** Leadership announced a model change but organizational execution did not match announcement. The distance between announcement and execution is observable.

**Alternative selection NOT made:** Full restructuring to achieve announced goal. Aggressive digital-first capital allocation.

## Kodak's Opaque Motivation (Zone A)

### Question 1: Why Sustained Film Protection Despite Margin Signal?

**Observable facts:**
- Margin collapse was visible (internal financials clear)
- Film decline was visible (internal forecasts documented)
- Digital potential was recognized (R&D investment proves recognition)

**Unobservable:**
- Did leadership genuinely believe film would recover?
- Was film protection driven by inertia / organizational resistance?
- Were sunk costs in film manufacturing preventing pivot?
- Were customer relationships (enterprise, government) locking Kodak into film revenue protection?

**Current Kodak classification:** "Response lag" — leadership saw problem but acted slowly

**PP#002 reframing:** Did leadership face an **observable selection problem** (film vs. digital) where the motivation for the selection remains **opaque**?

### Evidence of Selection Opacity

**Smoking gun locations (would bridge Zone A if found):**
1. Board minutes 1995-2005: Did board explicitly discuss choosing film protection over digital pivot? Did they justify the choice?
2. CEO strategy memos 2000-2005: Did Kodak's CEO write about the film-digital trade-off and articulate the rationale?
3. Capital allocation decisions: Was the decision to maintain film manufacturing capacity vs. expand digital a deliberate strategic choice (H4) or organizational constraint (H5)?

**Current status:** Not accessed. Kodak is bankrupt and private archives may not be public. But the structure is identical to BlackBerry's BBM question: organizational selection visible, motivation opaque.

## Kodak Evidence Classification — Evidence Class Taxonomy Application

### Class A — Structural Evidence

**What happened?**

Kodak invented digital camera (1975). Invested heavily in digital (1993-2005, $3B+). Announced digital-first strategy (2005). Failed to execute acceleration (digital <10% revenue through 2010). Margins collapsed (20% → 5%) during transition. Filed bankruptcy (2012).

**Confidence:** High
**Recoverable from:** Contemporaneous reporting, SEC filings, public record
**Bridges motivation gap:** No

**Example primary sources:**
- Kodak 10-K filings 2000-2012 (SEC documents)
- Kodak investor relations presentations (digital strategy statements)
- Kodak earnings call transcripts (leadership commentary on margin pressure)
- Trade press coverage (strategy announcements, delays)

### Class B — Retrospective Explanation

**Why participants later say it happened**

Post-bankruptcy interviews with Kodak executives could explain:
- Why digital investment didn't scale
- Whether leadership believed in digital or were protecting film
- What organizational barriers existed

**Confidence:** Medium (outcome-contaminated; decision-makers know bankruptcy outcome)
**Recoverable from:** Post-2012 interviews, memoirs, oral histories
**Outcome contamination risk:** High—executives may rationalize decisions through lens of failure

**Example potential sources:**
- Kodak executives interviewed by business historians 2013+ (if available)
- Memoirs written post-bankruptcy
- Harvard Business School case study post-mortems

### Class C — Contemporaneous Motivation

**Why decision-makers believed it at time T**

Board minutes 1995-2005 discussing film vs. digital capital allocation. CEO strategy memos articulating the film-protection rationale before bankruptcy outcome was known.

**Confidence:** Would be very high (only evidence class that genuinely bridges Zone A)
**Recoverable from:** Company archives, board records, internal correspondence
**Status:** Likely exists but not publicly accessible (Kodak bankruptcy didn't result in public archive release like government institutions)

**Example needed:**
- 1998 board memo: "Strategic rationale for maintaining film revenue base despite digital emergence"
- 2000 CEO strategy note: "Why digital acceleration would harm existing profit pool"
- 2005 capital committee decision: "Approved film protection budget over digital expansion"
