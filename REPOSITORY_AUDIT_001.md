## Surviving Objects — Inventory

### Scars (Wounds to Hypotheses, Narrowings, Contradictions)

A scar is an observation that contradicts or constrains a hypothesis.

#### Scar #1: Industry-Wide Blindness Weak Across Multiple Cases

**Definition:** H1 ("Signal weak, industry-wide blindness") loses pressure when evidence shows threat recognition at decision-maker level.

**Kodak case:** Not explicitly tested (Kodak focuses on response lag, not recognition)

**Nokia case:** Scar established. Threat recognition visible across industry:
- Samsung OHA adoption (November 2007)
- Nokia's public statement of concerns
- Analyst coverage of smartphone threat

**BlackBerry case:** Scar strengthened. Threat recognition at founder level:
- Lazaridis private implication (Summer 2007): "competing with a Mac, not a Nokia"
- Balsillie public response (February 2007): "not a sea-change" — contradicts blindness claim
- Both co-CEOs explicitly processing threat across 2007-2012

**Status:** Active. H1 carries substantially negative pressure across at least two independent cases. Not yet dead (Kodak did not specifically test recognition level), but weakened.

---

#### Scar #2: External Discontinuity Weak When Threat Was Visible in Real Time

**Definition:** H2 ("External discontinuity") loses pressure when evidence shows participants recognized and acted upon the threat contemporaneously, not after surprise external shock.

**Nokia case:** Partial scar. Samsung recognized OHA importance and committed early (Nov 2007). Nokia declined. Not a blind external shock; a visible choice.

**BlackBerry case:** Scar strengthened. Market data on iPhone adoption was public and observable:
- PlayBook announced Sept 2010 — same year iPad launched (April 2010)
- Leadership had access to market data and explicitly processed it
- Strategic decisions encoded the available information (PlayBook as enterprise-extension, not consumer pivot)

**Status:** Active. H2 carries negative pressure; the threat was visible and acted upon, not blind external shock. Scar holds across two independent cases.

---

#### Scar #3: Model Persistence Observable When Contradictory Feedback Accumulated

**Definition:** A scar against H4 (Model Failure) would be evidence that the model was revised after receiving contradictory feedback. A scar FOR H4 is evidence that the model persisted despite contradictory feedback.

**BlackBerry case:** Scar established and strengthened.
- Storm failure 2009: Model not revised; divergent responses instead
- Market share collapse 2010-2011: Model not revised; defensive responses instead
- PlayBook launch 2011: Model persisted; product encoded existing model despite contradictory market evidence
- Lazaridis April 2011: Model defended ("don't appreciate us"), not reconsidered
- Heins January 2012: Model explicitly confirmed ("no drastic change needed")

**Status:** Active and strong. Single case (BlackBerry) but accumulated evidence across multiple data points shows consistent model persistence. H4 gains pressure; model failure appears to be a useful category for BlackBerry.

---

#### Scar #4: Alternative Model Generation and Defeat (H5-Supporting Observation)

**Definition:** A scar FOR H5 (Organizational Constraint) is evidence that an alternative model existed but was structurally defeated.

**BlackBerry case:** Scar established and documented.
- Alternative model existed: BBM cross-platform / SMS 2.0 platform-licensing model
- Formally developed: Balsillie's proposal, pitched to carriers
- Defeated: Killed by Heins + Lazaridis coalition within weeks of CEO transition
- Mechanism observable: "focus on BB10, couldn't be distracted" — institutional defense of existing model

**Status:** Active. Single case, but observable mechanism of institutional selection against alternative. H5 gains regained force after Artifact #13 challenge.

### Constraints (Objects That Cannot Be True)

A constraint is an observation that rules out entire categories of explanation.

#### Constraint #1: Signal Absence Cannot Explain Kodak Failure

**Established:** Yes

**Evidence:** Kodak explicitly identified digital threat in 1975 (invented digital camera), invested heavily from 1993 onward, and tracked film decline visibly from 2000 onward. By 2005, CEO announced explicit pivot: "Digital revenue to exceed traditional."

**What it eliminates:** "Kodak didn't see it coming" as primary explanation

**What remains:**
- Why investment did not translate to competitive market position
- Why response lag persisted despite recognition
- Whether lag was structural choice or structural impossibility

**Status:** Hard constraint. Kodak did not fail from signal absence.

---

#### Constraint #2: Enterprise Customer Lock-in Does Not Automatically Drive Model Persistence

**Established:** Partial

**Evidence:** BlackBerry had both (A) enterprise customer relationships and (B) model persistence. But the causal direction is not established. The organization could have:
- Genuinely believed enterprise strategy was correct (H4)
- Been structurally constrained by enterprise relationships (H5)
- Both simultaneously

**What it eliminates:** Simple "enterprise lock-in explains everything" framing. The constraint exists but does not resolve why leadership persisted in enterprise strategy.

**What remains:**
- Whether enterprise constraint was sufficient to prevent pivoting
- Whether an alternative (BBM-as-platform) was genuinely viable
- Why Lazaridis supported killing the alternative even after private 2007 recognition of Mac-level threat

**Status:** Partial constraint. Enterprise relationships are load-bearing but not sufficient explanation.

---

#### Constraint #3: Blindness Hypothesis Exhausted Across Multiple Cases

**Established:** Yes

**Evidence:**
- Kodak: Explicit recognition documented
- Nokia: Industry recognition documented
- BlackBerry: Co-CEO level recognition documented

**What it eliminates:** "The industry didn't see it coming" across three independent cases. The blindness does not hold as a primary explanation at any of these sites.

**What remains:**
- Why recognition did not translate to proportional action (Kodak, BlackBerry)
- Why different competitors made different strategic choices (Nokia, BlackBerry)
- Whether recognition implies understanding (evidence-class problem: information without model integration)

**Status:** Hard constraint. H1 is substantially pressured; not the leading hypothesis in any active case.

### Pressure Points (Potentially Falsifiable Observations)

A pressure point is an observation that could be wounded or strengthened by additional evidence.

#### Pressure Point #001 — Backward Excavation

**Definition:** Investigations tend to push causal reasoning backward from outcome toward earlier decision stages.

**Observable pattern:**
- Kodak: → Margin signal (outcome) → Cost structure (stage) → R&D allocation failure (earlier stage) → Strategic hesitation (earlier still)
- GE: → Dividend cut (outcome) → Model divergence (stage) → Information integration failure (earlier stage)
- BlackBerry: → Market share collapse (outcome) → Model persistence (stage) → Decision mechanisms (earlier stages)

**Falsification condition:** If an investigation finds outcome-irrelevant causal chains (problems identified that were not load-bearing to the failure), PP#001's universality would weaken.

**Status:** Active and recurring. Pattern holds across three independent cases. Strengthened by diversity of case types (manufacturing, conglomerate, mobile hardware).

**Current probability:** High. Backward excavation appears to be a reliable observation-gathering mechanism, not an artifact of investigation design.

---

#### Pressure Point #002 — Observable Selection, Opaque Motivation

**Definition:** An organizational selection (choice between alternatives) may be structurally observable while the motivation for that selection remains unrecoverable from available public evidence.

**Observable pattern:**

| Case | Observable | Opaque |
|------|-----------|--------|
| GE #004 | Configuration visible (risk model structure, asset holdings) | Interpretation opaque (whether Immelt understood internal risk model) |
| BlackBerry #005 | Selection visible (alternative existed, was considered, was rejected) | Motivation opaque (why Lazaridis supported killing BBM despite recognizing Mac-level threat in 2007) |

**Specific evidence of Zone A opacity:**
- Lazaridis, when questioned about the rejection of BBM (Globe and Mail, September 2013): "declined to speak about board matters"
- Primary decision-maker explicitly available but chose not to provide motivation
- Difference from evidence-gap: This is active non-disclosure, not lost documentation

**Falsification conditions:**
- **Strong weakening:** Class C document (contemporaneous board memo, internal email at time of decision) explaining Lazaridis's rationale for rejecting BBM. Single strong Class C example would wound PP#002 directly.
- **Moderate weakening:** Multiple post-BlackBerry interviews with Lazaridis where he explains the decision. Class B evidence can provide motivation context, though outcome-contaminated.
- **Survival:** Continued absence of Class C; Lazaridis continues to decline speaking about board matters; no future disclosure emerges.

**Status:** Active and attacking-ready. Two independent cases provide structural support. Zone A/B boundary mapped. Three evidence classes define what can/cannot bridge the gap. PP#002 now has specific falsification condition.

**Cross-case support:** Strongest when observed independently (not following Backward Excavation hunting pattern from prior case). Waiting to see if future cases independently produce this pattern.

### Measurement Instruments (Reusable Frameworks)

A measurement instrument is a reusable framework for evaluating evidence across future cases, not specific to any single investigation.

#### Measurement Instrument #1 — Evidence Class Taxonomy

**Definition:** Classification of what evidence types are available for organizational decisions.

**Class A — Structural Evidence**

*Question answered: What happened?*

Characteristics:
- Proposal exists / proposal reviewed / proposal rejected
- High confidence
- Recoverable from contemporaneous reporting
- Does NOT bridge motivation gap

Examples:
- BBM cross-platform formally developed, pitched to carriers, killed by Heins + Lazaridis
- PlayBook launched April 2011 without native email
- Lazaridis "competing with a Mac" recognized privately 2007

**Class B — Retrospective Explanation**

*Question answered: Why participants later say it happened*

Characteristics:
- Interviews years after event
- Memoirs, post-hoc recollections
- Outcome-contaminated (decision-maker knows outcome when explaining)
- Outcome knowledge may have restructured memory of motivation
- Cannot be checked against conditions at time T

Examples:
- Lazaridis 2013 post-BlackBerry retrospectives (if they existed)
- Balsillie's September 2013 Globe and Mail statement on why he left board

**Class C — Contemporaneous Motivation**

*Question answered: Why decision-makers believed it at time T*

Characteristics:
- Board memos, internal emails, strategy notes
- Decision rationale written before outcome known
- Only evidence class that genuinely bridges Zone A
- Rare
- Can be checked against contemporaneous conditions
- Produced before outcome knowledge contaminated memory

Examples:
- A January 2012 board memo stating reasoning for rejecting BBM (does not currently exist in accessible sources)
- Internal strategy emails 2011 discussing PlayBook design rationale
- GE board materials 2014-2016 discussing power market assumptions

**Deployment status:** Ready for all future investigations. Should be applied to all cases before claiming resolution at the motivation level.

**Critical discipline:** A finding based on Class B should be explicitly marked as outcome-contaminated. Only Class C can establish genuine contemporaneous rationale. Failure to classify evidence type is a failure to understand what is actually being claimed.

---

#### Measurement Instrument #2 — Zone A/B Boundary Mapping

**Definition:** Systematic distinction between what organizational behavior is observable and what motivation remains opaque.

**Zone B — Observable Structure**

What can be documented:
- Decisions made
- Alternatives considered and rejected
- Timing of decisions
- Stated rationale for decisions
- Organizational coalitions supporting/opposing choices

What cannot be:
- Why individuals held beliefs
- Whether stated rationale matches actual motivation
- Whether decision-maker privately doubted the choice

**Zone A — Opaque Motivation**

What remains unrecoverable from public evidence:
- Internal conviction vs. structural constraint
- Genuine belief vs. post-hoc rationalization
- Preference ordering when forced choices occurred
- Why reasonable people chose differently in same situation

**Deployment status:** Ready for all future investigations. Should explicitly map Zone A/B boundary before claiming to have explained organizational failure.

**Critical finding:** Organizations may be understandable at the level of their selections without being understandable at the level of their motivations. And that level of understanding may be precise enough to be useful while remaining incomplete enough to be honest about its limits.

## Objects That Died (Contradicted by Evidence)

### Dead Object #1: "Disruption Invisibility"

**Original form:** Technology disruptions are invisible to incumbents until too late.

**Killed by:** All five cases

**Evidence:**
- Kodak: Disruption visible; recognition documented 1975+; investment substantial
- Nokia: Disruption visible to Samsung; OHA decision was conscious choice
- BlackBerry: Disruption visible to Lazaridis (2007 private implication); explicit strategic responses initiated
- GE: Disruption visible to Flannery; Immelt's model was active choice, not blindness
- WeWork: Not yet investigated

**Status:** Dead. All cases contradict the invisibility thesis. Disruption visibility is not the problem. What to do with visible disruption is the problem.

## Active Hypotheses (Still Competitive Across Cases)

### H1 — Signal Visible, Response Lagged

**Status:** Weakened but not dead

**Where living:** Kodak (closure) shows strong evidence of response lag
**Where challenged:** Nokia (Samsung responded proportionally); BlackBerry (responses were proportional, just implemented wrong models); GE (Flannery arrived and pivoted rapidly)

**Current position:** May be case-specific rather than general. Kodak shows it clearly; other cases don't.

---

### H3 — Execution Failure

**Status:** Active, secondary

**Where living:** Kodak (R&D spending did not convert to market share); BlackBerry (Storm execution, PlayBook execution, BB10 failure); GE (complexity of execution prevented rapid re-response)

**Complication:** Execution failure can be downstream of model failure. BlackBerry might have executed BB10 well; it was the model that was wrong. Cannot separate yet.

---

### H4 — Model Failure (Incorrect Demand Model)

**Status:** Leading in BlackBerry; active in GE; unclear in Nokia and Kodak

**Where strongest:** BlackBerry. Model persistence documented despite contradictory feedback; alternative killed; zone A opaque but zone B clear.

**Where contested:** GE. H2-C (Information Integration Failure) is the pure form. Awaiting evidence access.

---

### H5 — Organizational Constraint

**Status:** Active, entangled with H4

**Where strongest:** BlackBerry. Alternative model existed and lost; Balsillie's departure direct consequence; institutional coalition preserved existing model.

**Entanglement point:** Cannot distinguish whether BB10 focus reflects genuine belief (H4) or structural defense of existing model (H5) without access to Zone A.

## Meta-Level Observation

**The repository is generating boundaries faster than explanations.**

After five investigations:
- Constraints: 3 (all high confidence, all negative findings — what's NOT true)
- Pressure points: 2 (both falsifiable, both active)
- Measurement instruments: 2 (both reusable, both deployed)
- Surviving hypotheses: 4 (H1, H3, H4, H5; none established as universal)

This is a healthy asymmetry for early-stage research.

Boundaries are harder to fake than explanations. An established boundary can remain useful even if the explanations change. The repository should continue accumulating boundaries before settling on explanations.
