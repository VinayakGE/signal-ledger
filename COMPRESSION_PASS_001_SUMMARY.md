## Finding #1: PP#002 Is Portfolio Artifact

**Pattern:** Observable organizational selection paired with opaque motivation

### Three-Case Evidence

**Kodak #001:**
- Observable: Film vs. digital capital allocation divergence documented (R&D investment $3B+ vs. film-revenue protection)
- Opaque: Why film protection was chosen over digital acceleration (conviction vs. constraint unknown)
- Structure: Margin collapse visible; strategic motivation for margin decay pattern unknown

**BlackBerry #005:**
- Observable: BBM cross-platform alternative proposed, developed, pitched to carriers, killed by institutional coalition
- Opaque: Why Lazaridis supported rejection (despite 2007 private "competing with a Mac" recognition)
- Structure: Primary decision-maker available but "declined to speak about board matters" (active non-disclosure)

**GE #004:**
- Observable: Power segment capital allocation; Immelt's model vs. Flannery's revised assumptions
- Opaque: Whether Immelt's divergence was information gap (H2-A) or interpretation choice (H4)
- Structure: Model divergence documented; contemporaneous reasoning for Immelt's choices unclear

### Pattern Strength

**Cross-case recurring structure:**
- All three cases show observable organizational choice
- All three cases show decision between documented alternatives (film vs. digital; BBM platform vs. BB10; Immelt model vs. Flannery model)
- All three cases show motivation remaining opaque despite primary sources being available (Kodak exec interviews possible; Lazaridis declined; Immelt interviews outcome-contaminated)

**Assessment:** PP#002 is **confirmed portfolio artifact**, not case-specific observation

**Implication:** The boundary between observable structure and opaque motivation is not an artifact of investigation design. It appears independently across different domains (consumer photography, mobile hardware, conglomerate). This is a structural property of organizational complexity.

## Finding #2: Zone A/B Boundary Is Portfolio Artifact

**Pattern:** Organizational behavior observable (Zone B) while motivation unobservable (Zone A)

### Two-Case Evidence

**Kodak #001:**
- Zone B (Observable): Margin collapse 20% → 5%; R&D investment $3B+; revenue flat $10B; strategic announcement 2005 vs. execution gap
- Zone A (Opaque): Why film protection sustained despite margin signal; why R&D didn't convert to revenue; whether digital strategy was genuine priority

**BlackBerry #005:**
- Zone B (Observable): BBM proposal, rejection mechanism, Balsillie's departure consequence documented
- Zone A (Opaque): Why Lazaridis chose BB10 over BBM platform; whether choice reflects conviction or institutional constraint

### Structure Validation

Both cases show identical structure:
- What the organization did is recoverable from public evidence
- Why the organization did it is not recoverable from public evidence

**Assessment:** Zone A/B boundary is **confirmed portfolio artifact**

**Implication:** Organizations can be understood as systems of observable selections without understanding motivations. This is not a research limitation; it's a boundary. Acknowledging the boundary is more honest than inferring motivation.

## Finding #3: Evidence Class Taxonomy Clarifies Portfolio State

**Pattern:** Systematic evidence distribution across cases reveals epistemic boundary

### Portfolio Evidence Distribution

**Class A (Structural Evidence) — Strong**

All five cases have clear, well-documented observable structure:
- Kodak: Financial signals, investment facts, outcome
- Nokia: Market data, strategic choices, competitive outcomes
- WeWork: Public investor decks, narrative documents, outcome
- GE: Corporate events, capital allocation, strategic decisions
- BlackBerry: Product facts, strategic choices, outcomes

**Portfolio strength at Class A:** ✓ Strong. Can reliably establish "what happened" in any case.

---

**Class B (Retrospective Evidence) — Systematically Weak**

All five cases suffer from outcome contamination when retrospective evidence is available:
- Kodak: Executives explain post-bankruptcy; outcome-aware
- Nokia: Elop retrospectives dominate; survivor narrative bias; pre-2007 decision-makers unavailable
- WeWork: Recent; outcome-aware narratives
- GE: Immelt explains post-dividend-cut; Flannery explains as "fixer"
- BlackBerry: Lazaridis declined; Balsillie provides partial narrative

**Portfolio weakness at Class B:** ✗ Weak. Outcome contamination is systematic. Cannot establish contemporaneous beliefs reliably.

---

**Class C (Contemporaneous Motivation) — Systematically Inaccessible**

All five cases show Class C evidence either unavailable or blocked:
- Kodak: Board materials not public (defunct company)
- Nokia: Board-level reasoning private (public company maintains privacy)
- WeWork: Likely not documented; private company archives uncertain
- GE: Blocked by network policy (environment cannot access SEC, board materials)
- BlackBerry: Not found; primary source actively protected ("declined to speak")

**Portfolio constraint at Class C:** ✗ Inaccessible. Zone A (motivation) remains permanently opaque across portfolio.

---

### Critical Insight: Zone A Opacity Is Boundary, Not Failure

Class C evidence is not merely "hard to find." It is **systematically unavailable**:

1. **Defunct companies** (Kodak) don't preserve archives
2. **Public companies** (Nokia, GE) keep board materials private
3. **Private companies** (WeWork, BlackBerry) may not document decisions
4. **Primary sources** actively choose not to disclose (Lazaridis)

This pattern is **not random**. It's structural.

**Implication:** Zone A opacity is a legitimate epistemic boundary. Researchers should:
- Expect Class C evidence to be rare
- Accept that motivation often remains unrecoverable
- Distinguish "we couldn't find the evidence" from "the organization protected the evidence"
- Value Class A evidence for what it can establish without requiring Class C

## What Compression Revealed About Phase 2 Doctrine

**Original Phase 2 Doctrine stated:**
> Ask what survived investigation, not what was investigated.

**Compression Pass revealed this means:**

1. **Inventory:** What objects exist? (Done in Audit #001)
2. **Validation:** Do those objects hold across the full portfolio? (Done in Compression Pass #001)
3. **Then decide:** Which direction maximizes learning?

The compression step is not optional. It transforms "here's what we found" into "here's what actually holds."

With compression:
- PP#002 is portfolio artifact (appears independently in 3 cases)

**The difference is knowing what's real vs. what's pattern-matching.**
